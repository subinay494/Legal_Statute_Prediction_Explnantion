In this case Trade Union rivalry between INTUC and CITU on the one hand and BMS on the other culminated into the atrocious incident of April 21, 1983 in which one Thanukuttan @ Nanukuttan died and three persons PW-1, PW-2, and PW-4, suffered injuries.
In respect of this incident the police filed charge- sheet against the appellants and twenty other persons of whom A-13 dies and the remaining were tried on the following facts for offences under Sections 120B, 143, 147, 148, 149, 324, 307, 302 and 109 of the Indian Penal Code, 1860.
and Section 3 of the [Explosive Substances Act, 1908](http://www.liiofindia.org/in/legis/cen/num_act/esa1908252/).
The headload-workers employed in the industrial Estate of Pappanamcode are members of either INTUC or CITU (hereinafter referred to as 'the complainant group') whereas 'the accused group' belongs to BMS union.
<S1>The members of 'the complainant group' were preventing the members of 'the accused group' from working in the Industrial Estate.</S1>
<S1>For this reason accused 1 to 20 of the accused group hatched a conspiracy to murder the headload-workers of the complaint group pursuant to which A-23 had agreed to supply the country made bombs on April 20, On the morning of April 21, 1983, accused A-21 and A-22 took the bombs to a bylane at Vettukuzhi near the Pappanamcode Industrial Estate and gave them to A-1 at 15 a.m.</S1>

<S1>D-1 and D-1(a) on which the trial court placed reliance are to the effect that the deceased was sitting at the time of the incident whereas in the evidence given in Court it was stated that he was sleeping.</S1>
<S1>The aforesaid contradictions are hardly material to decide whether he died of a homicidal death at the place of the incident.</S1>
<S1>While considering point no.1, the trial court accepted the testimony of PWs-1 to 5 and 7 on the ground that the same was corroborated from the evidence of PWs 8 and It also relied on the evidence of PWs 8 and 9 in coming to the conclusion that the deceased sustained fatal injuries at the slanting platform in front of the General Metals in the Industrial Estate at Pappanamcode.</S1>
<S1>From that evidence, the conclusion that A-2 threw bomb at the deceased who was seriously injured and died of a homicidal death, was irresistible but the trial court held otherwise which is patently untenable.</S1>
<S1>We have gone through the evidence of eye witnesses, PWs 1 to 5 and 7; their presence on the scene of occurrence cannot be doubted because PWs-1, 2 and 4 are injured witnesses and their names are also found in the FIR (Exh.</S1>
P-1).
Further, PWs.
<S1>8 and 9 are independent witnesses; though they do not speak of the presence of the accused on the scene of occurrence that is for the reason that they came after the accused and some of the injured persons had fled from the scene of occurrence, they, however, spoke that they had seen PWs.3 and 4 on the scene of occurrence.</S1>
<S1>From their testimony, it becomes evident that the deceased and some others were taking rest on the concrete platform in front of General Metals.</S1>
<S1>While one of them was reading magazine 'Kumari' and others were hearing, they noticed magazine 'Kumari' and others were hearing, they noticed that A-1 was having something in the shape of the tennis ball which was wrapped in a paper and which was thrown by him at PW-1; this was followed by throwing of similar object by A-2 on the deceased, which hit him on his stomach and his intestine came out.</S1>
<S1>Immediately thereafter, the third bomb was thrown by A-3 which fell amidst them.</S1>
<S1>It is nobody's case that at the time of occurrence, persons other than the members of the rival unions were present there.</S1>
<S1>After hearing the sound of explosion, some persons no doubt came to the scene of occurrence, of whom PWs 8 and 9 have been examined and obviously they have spoken to the facts which they noticed only after the explosion of the bombs.</S1>
<S1>The other witnesses, if any, would have been of no avail to the defence nor were they necessary for purposes of establishing the quilt of the accused.</S1>
<S1>Therefore, union rivalry would not be a ground to brush aside their evidence after having found that the same is consistent and truthful.</S1>
<S1>It is no doubt true that throwing of bomb is a sudden act but the witnesses have clearly stated from which direction the accused came and who among them threw the country-made bombs and who hit with bricks and sticks.</S1>
<S1>It is not a case where the incident took place in wee hour of the night when everybody was sleeping and then they got up after the explosion and would not have been in a position to see the actual throwing of bombs.</S1>
<S1>They were all sitting on the platform and were obviously conscious of what was happening in the surrounding as is evident from their statements.</S1>
<S1>We also find no substance in the issue as to whether the deceased was sitting or lying - a fact which weighed with the trial court to disbelieve the prosecution story.</S1>
<S1>It is quite possible that the witnesses who stated that Nanukuttan was sitting must have seen him before he lied down on the platform and the witnesses who stated that he was sleeping must have seen him lying and stated that he was sleeping.</S1>
This contradiction, in our view, does not affect the prosecution case.
